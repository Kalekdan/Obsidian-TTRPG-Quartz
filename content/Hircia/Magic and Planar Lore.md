Magic is everywhere in Hircia, but it is not used the same way everywhere. Some places treat it like a science, some like a craft, and some like a religious or dangerous force.
## Core ideas
### Magic is ambient
Magic exists across the world rather than being limited to isolated wizard towers or sacred sites. Different cultures simply relate to it differently.

### The planes matter
Hircia’s history is shaped by the boundaries between planes. Several major events hinge on attempts to cross, weaken, or close those boundaries. These include:
- The attempt by [[Jotunheimr]] to manipulate the plains
- [[Surzak]] tearing through the planes during the events of [[The Isles of Fate]]
- The yearly festival in [[Virellis]] (see [[The Festival of the Dead]])
## Major magical eras
### Jotunheimr at its prime
Jotunheimr is remembered as a highly advanced magical civilisation. Its people are said to have had an unusually deep understanding of the planes and of how to shape the land with magic. They considered magic a science, and studied it as such.

### The Isles of Fate and the Convergence
The attempt to tear down planar boundaries to resurrect the dead leads to one of the largest magical disasters in the setting. The Convergence then leaves monsters and other planar intrusions embedded in the world.

## How magic looks in different places
### Scientific magic
Pinapota represents the more formal, technical side of magic. It treats magical study almost like an academic discipline.
### Practical craft magic
Other places use magic more casually or artistically. Roseport is a good example of a location where magic feels woven into daily life.

## Planar notes
* Planar boundaries can (and do) weaken.
* Attempts to control them can backfire spectacularly.
* The Convergence leaves a long tail of unstable, cross-planar reality.
* Post-Convergence Hircia should be treated as a world with more monsters, more uncertainty, and fewer clean borders between things.
